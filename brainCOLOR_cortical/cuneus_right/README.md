
# cuneus

## Overview

The right cuneus is a medial occipital lobe region of the cerebral cortex, forming part of the dorsal visual system and located between the calcarine sulcus inferiorly and the parieto-occipital sulcus superiorly on the right hemisphere. It primarily encompasses portions of early visual areas (notably V1 and V2), receiving highly organized retinotopic input from the lateral geniculate nucleus via the optic radiations, with a bias toward processing visual information from the contralateral (left) lower visual field. Functionally, the right cuneus is involved in basic visual feature analysis (such as orientation, contrast, and spatial frequency), visual attention, and integration of visual signals for spatial orientation and visuomotor coordination; it also participates in higher-order visual cognition through its connectivity with parietal and frontal regions. The right cuneus is part of the broader occipital cortex and can be structurally and functionally delineated in atlases such as brainCOLOR, which partition it as a distinct cortical region within standardized neuroimaging spaces. There is no direct Wikipedia page for “Right cuneus (brainCOLOR)” as an atlas-specific region; a related and encompassing structure with a Wikipedia entry is the cuneus of the occipital lobe: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuneus.
