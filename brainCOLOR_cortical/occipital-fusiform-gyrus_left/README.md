
# occipital-fusiform-gyrus

## Overview

The left occipital-fusiform gyrus is a ventral occipitotemporal cortical region spanning the junction of the occipital and fusiform (lateral occipitotemporal) gyri, typically corresponding to portions of Brodmann areas 18/19 and 37. It lies on the basal surface of the left hemisphere, medial to the inferior temporal gyrus and lateral to the lingual and parahippocampal gyri, and is supplied primarily by branches of the posterior cerebral artery. Functionally, this region participates in high-level visual processing, including object and face recognition, word and letter-form processing, and integration of shape, color, and texture information; it forms part of the ventral “what” visual pathway and interacts with language and memory-related temporal lobe structures. The brainCOLOR Atlas naming emphasizes its anatomical location at the interface of the occipital cortex and fusiform gyrus, a zone often implicated in category-selective visual processing such as the visual word form area and fusiform face area, though these subregions may be more precisely localized within broader occipitotemporal cortex.
