
# Amygdala

## Overview

The right amygdala is an almond-shaped nucleus complex located in the medial temporal lobe, anterior to the hippocampus and adjacent to the temporal horn of the lateral ventricle, and is part of the limbic system. It consists of multiple subnuclei (including basolateral, centromedial, and cortical groups) that integrate sensory inputs, contextual information, and internal states to modulate emotional processing, particularly fear, threat detection, and emotional salience, as well as aspects of autonomic and endocrine responses via projections to the hypothalamus and brainstem. Functionally, the right amygdala has been associated with rapid, coarse evaluation of emotional stimuli and may show lateralized involvement in processing facial expressions, negative affect, and emotional memory, with strong reciprocal connections to the prefrontal cortex, hippocampal formation, and sensory association cortices.  

There is no direct Wikipedia page specifically for the “Right Amygdala” as a separate structure; a related and encompassing entry is the general amygdala page: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amygdala
