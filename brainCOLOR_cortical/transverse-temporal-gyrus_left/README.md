
# transverse-temporal-gyrus

## Overview

The left transverse temporal gyrus, also known as Heschl’s gyrus, is a primary auditory cortical region located on the superior surface of the temporal lobe within the lateral (Sylvian) fissure, forming part of the superior temporal plane. It constitutes the core of the primary auditory cortex (Brodmann areas 41 and parts of 42), receiving highly organized, tonotopic input from the medial geniculate nucleus of the thalamus. Neurons in this region are specialized for basic acoustic feature processing, including sound frequency, intensity, and temporal structure, and support early stages of speech and complex sound analysis. The left hemisphere transverse temporal gyrus is particularly implicated in language-related auditory processing, contributing to the decoding of phonetic and temporal cues essential for speech perception and integration with higher-order language areas in the superior temporal and frontal cortices.
