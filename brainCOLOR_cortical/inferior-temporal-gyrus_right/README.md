
# inferior-temporal-gyrus

## Overview

The right inferior temporal gyrus is a longitudinal cortical region on the ventral aspect of the right temporal lobe, situated between the middle temporal gyrus superiorly and the occipitotemporal (fusiform) gyrus medially, extending from the temporal pole anteriorly toward the occipital lobe posteriorly. It is composed primarily of neocortex and participates in high-level visual processing, including object recognition, complex shape analysis, and aspects of visual memory, with right-hemispheric contributions often linked to nonverbal and visuospatial functions. Its connectivity includes reciprocal projections with visual association areas in the occipital and parietal lobes, limbic structures involved in memory and emotion, and prefrontal regions implicated in attention and decision-making about visual stimuli. The right inferior temporal gyrus is part of the ventral “what” visual stream and is supplied mainly by branches of the inferior division of the middle cerebral artery and, posteriorly, by branches of the posterior cerebral artery. There is no direct Wikipedia page for the “Right inferior temporal gyrus” as a separate structure; a related page describing the inferior temporal gyrus in general is: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inferior_temporal_gyrus.
