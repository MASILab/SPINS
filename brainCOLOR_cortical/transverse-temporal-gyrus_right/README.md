
# transverse-temporal-gyrus

## Overview

The right transverse temporal gyrus, also known as Heschl’s gyrus on the right hemisphere, is a short, obliquely oriented convolution located on the superior surface of the temporal lobe, buried within the lateral (Sylvian) fissure and forming part of the primary auditory cortex (Brodmann areas 41 and 42). It receives dense thalamocortical input from the medial geniculate nucleus and is crucial for early-stage processing of acoustic information, including sound frequency, intensity, and temporal features. While both hemispheres contribute to basic auditory perception, the right transverse temporal gyrus is often associated with processing aspects of nonverbal auditory stimuli such as music, pitch, and spectral patterns, and it participates in wider temporal-lobe networks for auditory scene analysis and integration with higher-order cognitive functions. There is no direct Wikipedia page for the “right transverse temporal gyrus” specifically; a closely related and encompassing structure is described here: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transverse_temporal_gyri.
