
# temporal-pole

## Overview

The left temporal pole is the most anterior portion of the left temporal lobe, forming the rostral end of the temporal cortex and overlying the temporal pole of the temporal bone. Cytoarchitectonically, it includes parts of Brodmann area 38 and is characterized by association cortex that integrates multimodal sensory inputs. It is heavily interconnected with limbic and paralimbic regions, including the amygdala, hippocampus, orbitofrontal cortex, and insula, and receives input from higher-order auditory and visual association areas. Functionally, the left temporal pole is implicated in semantic memory, object and person knowledge, language processing (particularly naming and word comprehension), and social-emotional cognition. Lesions in this region, such as in semantic variant primary progressive aphasia or anterior temporal lobectomy, are associated with deficits in semantic knowledge, naming, and in some cases socio-emotional processing. There is no direct Wikipedia page for the “left temporal pole” as a distinct entry; a related and encompassing structure is the temporal pole: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temporal_pole.
