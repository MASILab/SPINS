
# fusiform-gyrus

## Overview

The left fusiform gyrus is a ventral temporal lobe structure located on the basal surface of the cerebral hemisphere, extending longitudinally between the inferior temporal gyrus laterally and the parahippocampal gyrus medially, and demarcated by the occipitotemporal sulcus and collateral sulcus. Cytoarchitectonically, it encompasses multiple visual association areas involved in high-level visual processing, including object recognition, face perception (e.g., the fusiform face area), and word/letter-form recognition, with strong left-hemispheric specialization for visual word form processing and reading. It is heavily interconnected with occipital visual cortices, anterior temporal regions, and frontal language networks, supporting the integration of visual inputs with semantic and linguistic representations. Lesions in the left fusiform gyrus can produce deficits such as pure alexia, impaired visual word recognition, and category-specific recognition disturbances, reflecting its role in transforming complex visual patterns into abstract, language-relevant codes.
