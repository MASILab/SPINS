
# posterior-cingulate-gyrus

## Overview

The left posterior cingulate gyrus is a cortical region located on the medial surface of the left cerebral hemisphere, forming part of the posterior segment of the cingulate cortex within the limbic lobe. It lies dorsal to the corpus callosum, posterior to the mid-cingulate area, and extends toward the precuneus and retrosplenial cortex. Cytoarchitectonically, it is generally associated with posterior portions of Brodmann areas 23 and 31. Functionally, the posterior cingulate gyrus is a core hub of the default mode network, implicated in internally directed cognition, autobiographical and episodic memory retrieval, self-referential processing, and integration of emotional and sensory information. It has dense reciprocal connections with the medial temporal lobe (including hippocampal formation), medial prefrontal cortex, and parietal association areas, and shows marked metabolic activity at rest and altered activity in various neuropsychiatric and neurodegenerative conditions (for example, Alzheimer’s disease).  

There is no direct Wikipedia page specifically for the “left posterior cingulate gyrus”; a closely related and encompassing structure is the posterior cingulate cortex: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Posterior_cingulate_cortex
