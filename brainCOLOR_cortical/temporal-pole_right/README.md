
# temporal-pole

## Overview

The right temporal pole is the most anterior portion of the right temporal lobe, situated rostral to the superior, middle, and inferior temporal gyri and adjacent to the anterior tip of the temporal horn of the lateral ventricle. Cytoarchitectonically, it includes areas corresponding largely to Brodmann area 38 and exhibits transitional features between limbic and neocortical cortex. It is heavily interconnected with limbic structures (including the amygdala and hippocampus), orbitofrontal cortex, insula, and higher-order sensory association areas, forming part of networks supporting semantic memory, social cognition, and multimodal integration of emotional and sensory information. Functionally, it has been implicated in language and naming (particularly for proper nouns), processing of familiar faces and social concepts, and the emotional coloring of autobiographical memories; lesions or degeneration in this region, such as in semantic variant primary progressive aphasia or frontotemporal dementia, are associated with semantic impairments and changes in social-emotional behavior.
