
# superior-temporal-gyrus

## Overview

The left superior temporal gyrus (STG) is a cortical region located on the lateral surface of the temporal lobe, extending from the temporal pole posteriorly toward the parietal lobe and bounded superiorly by the lateral (Sylvian) fissure and inferiorly by the superior temporal sulcus. It is cytoarchitectonically heterogeneous, containing primary auditory cortex (Heschl’s gyrus, mainly in the transverse temporal gyri adjacent to the STG) and higher-order auditory association areas, and in the dominant (typically left) hemisphere it encompasses critical language-related zones such as Wernicke’s area in its posterior portion. Functionally, the left STG is involved in the perception and processing of complex sounds, including speech, phonological decoding, and aspects of lexical-semantic integration, as well as contributing to audiovisual speech perception and prosodic processing. It participates in large-scale networks for language comprehension, auditory working memory, and social communication, and shows characteristic lateralization patterns in most right-handed individuals.
